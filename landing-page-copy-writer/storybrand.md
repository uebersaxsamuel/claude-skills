# StoryBrand Framework — Reference

Based on "Building a StoryBrand" by Donald Miller.
Applied to landing pages, sales pages, and homepages.

---

## The Core Principle

**The customer is the hero of the story — not the company.**

When companies position themselves as the hero, customers lose interest
because they can't see themselves in the story.

The company = The Guide (Yoda, Gandalf, Morpheus)
The customer = The Hero (Luke Skywalker, Frodo, Neo)

---

## The SB7 Framework (7 Elements)

### 1. A Character (The Hero)
- Who is the hero? → The potential customer
- What do they want? → The external goal (concrete, specific)
- The page must immediately signal: "This page is for YOU"

**In practice:**
- Name the audience explicitly: "For coaches who..." / "If you're a Webflow freelancer..."
- Speak to the desire directly, not the product

---

### 2. Has a Problem (The Problem)
Customers don't buy solutions to external problems — they buy solutions
to their **internal problems** (the emotion behind it).

**3 Levels of the Problem:**

| Level | What it is | Example (Webflow Freelancer) |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **External** | The obvious symptom | "I'm not getting enough inquiries" |
| **Internal** | The emotion/frustration | "I feel insecure about whether my business will work" |
| **Philosophical** | The injustice | "Good work should be fairly compensated" |

**In practice:**
- Address all 3 levels
- Customers buy because of the internal problem — but they search for solutions to the external one
- The philosophical problem creates the emotional connection

---

### 3. Meets a Guide (The Guide)
The company positions itself as the guide — through two qualities:

**Empathy:** "I understand your pain" (emotional)
**Authority:** "I can help you" (rational/factual)

**WRONG:** "We are the leading agency for..."
**RIGHT:** "We've helped 200 freelancers double their rates."

**Demonstrating Authority:**
- Numbers: clients served, years, results achieved
- Logos: known clients or partners
- Awards, certifications
- Case studies with concrete outcomes

**Demonstrating Empathy:**
- Show that you know or have experienced the problem
- "We know how frustrating it is when..."
- Avoid corporate-speak

---

### 4. The Plan (The Plan)
Customers want to know what happens — uncertainty prevents buying decisions.

**Two Plan Types:**

**Process Plan** (reduces action anxiety)
→ "Here's how it works: 1. Fill out the form 2. We reach out within 24h 3. You get started"

**Agreement Plan** (reduces purchase anxiety)
→ Implicit promises: "No commitment", "Money-back guarantee", "Personal support"

**In practice:**
- Max. 3-4 steps (more = confusion)
- Keep steps so simple they don't trigger fear
- Place directly after the Guide/Trust section

---

### 5. Call to Action (Direct vs. Transitional)

**Direct CTA** → Calls for the purchase/signup/action
Examples: "Buy now", "Book a session", "Get access"
→ Use for the main CTA on the page (max. 1 type)

**Transitional CTA** → Keeps visitors who aren't ready to buy
Examples: "Download the free guide", "Watch the demo", "Read the case study"
→ Use for lead capture or secondary goal

**Important:**
- On a landing page, use only ONE CTA type consistently throughout
- Direct CTA always in the hero above the fold
- Repeat the CTA in navigation, hero, middle, and end of page

---

### 6. Avoiding Failure (The Stakes — Failure)
People are more motivated by fear of loss than by potential gain.

**Show what's at stake if the hero DOESN'T act:**
- Not as a threat — as a reality check
- Weave in subtly, usually in the problem section
- "Without [solution], you risk..."

**WRONG:** Fearmongering
**RIGHT:** Clearly and empathetically naming a realistic consequence

---

### 7. The Vision of Success (The Resolution)
Show life after the purchase — the desired outcome.

**3 Types of Success:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **External** | Concrete result | "30% more revenue" |
| **Internal** | Emotional state | "Finally present with confidence" |
| **Transformation** | Identity shift | "From freelancer to business owner" |

**In practice:**
- Show the success vision in the hero (headline) and at the end of the page
- Use testimonials as proof of the success vision
- Be specific: "500+ clients save an average of 6h/week"

---

## StoryBrand for Homepages vs. Landing Pages

### Homepage
- Can address multiple audiences broadly
- Guides visitors to more specific subpages / landing pages
- Hero: Can be slightly broader, but still a clear promise
- Navigation: May have more links than a pure landing page

### Landing Page (Campaign / Product)
- **One audience, one problem, one goal, one CTA**
- No distracting navigation
- Everything on the page leads to the one CTA
- Length: As long as necessary (short for simple decisions, long for high-risk purchases)

---

## Common StoryBrand Mistakes

❌ **Positioning the brand as the hero** → "We are the best..."
✅ Fix: "You will..." / "Your clients will..."

❌ **No clear promise in the hero** → Beautiful images, no substance
✅ Fix: Headline = the hero's transformation in one sentence

❌ **Too much information** → Overwhelms the visitor
✅ Fix: Less is more — one idea per section

❌ **No CTA or a weak one** → "Learn More" as the main CTA
✅ Fix: A direct, action-oriented CTA ("Get started now")

❌ **No clear plan** → Visitor doesn't know what happens after clicking
✅ Fix: Always include a 3-step plan

❌ **Missing stakes** → No pressure to act
✅ Fix: Briefly and empathetically name what happens without action

---

## Quick Reference: Page Structure by StoryBrand

```
HERO:         Who the hero is + What they want + Direct CTA
PROBLEM:      External + Internal + Philosophical pain
GUIDE:        Empathy + Authority (numbers, logos, testimonials)
PLAN:         3 simple steps
CTA:          Direct call to action
SUCCESS:      Life after the purchase / the success vision
STAKES:       What's at stake (subtle)
CTA:          Repeated at the end
```
